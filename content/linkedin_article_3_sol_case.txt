Last week, my agent flagged a SOL setup that exemplifies everything I've been writing about.

THE SETUP

Monday afternoon, 2:47 PM GMT. My monitoring system pinged:

→ Funding rate: -0.08% across major perp exchanges (excessive short bias)
→ Open Interest: +12% in 2 hours (new money entering short)
→ Volume: 2.5x average (institutional footprint)
→ Spot premium on Coinbase: 0.3% (buying pressure in regulated venues)

Classic short squeeze conditions. All criteria met.

THE HESITATION

I saw the alert. I pulled up the chart. I verified the data.

Then I hesitated.

"Let me see confirmation first."

I wanted to see the move start before committing. This seems reasonable—it's risk management, right?

THE COST

Four hours later: +14%.

I watched it happen from the sidelines. The setup was textbook. My conviction wasn't.

THE ANALYSIS

What went wrong?

1. I conflated "confirmation" with "certainty"
2. I let recent losses affect my willingness to take valid setups
3. I didn't size appropriately for my conviction level
4. I forgot that edge means winning over a series, not every trade

THE LESSON

This is why I'm building calibration systems, not just detection systems.

The agent did its job perfectly. It detected confluence. It flagged the edge case. It met all criteria in my framework.

The failure was in the human element: conviction at the moment of execution.

MOVING FORWARD

New rule: If it meets all criteria, I take it. No second-guessing.

The hesitation cost—12% worse fills on average, missed entries entirely—is higher than the occasional false positive.

Trust the system. Execute the plan. Review the results.

That's the job.
